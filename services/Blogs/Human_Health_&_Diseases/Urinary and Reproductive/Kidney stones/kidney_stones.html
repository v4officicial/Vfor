Kidney Stones | Causes, Symptoms & Treatment

Kidney stones are hard mineral deposits formed in the kidneys when urine becomes concentrated with minerals and salts. They can cause severe pain, urinary problems, and complications if they block urinary flow. Proper hydration, dietary modifications, and timely treatment are crucial to management and prevention.

Symptoms

Symptoms of kidney stones vary with size and location but include:

  • Severe, sharp pain in the side and back, below the ribs (renal colic)
  • Pain radiating to the lower abdomen and groin
  • Painful urination (dysuria)
  • Pink, red, or brown urine (hematuria)
  • Frequent urge to urinate or urinating small amounts
  • Nausea and vomiting
  • Fever and chills if infection is present

Causes

Kidney stones form due to:

  • Excess calcium, oxalate, or uric acid in urine
  • Dehydration reducing urine volume
  • Diet high in protein, sodium, or sugar
  • Family history and genetic predisposition
  • Medical conditions like hyperparathyroidism or gout
  • Certain medications that increase stone formation
  • Urinary tract infections creating struvite stones

Children

  • Kidney stones are rare but increasing in children
  • Associated with metabolic disorders, dehydration, or dietary factors

Adults

  • Predominantly affected age group with lifestyle and metabolic factors
  • More common in males, but incidence rising in females

Aged People

  • Aging may affect risk factors but overall prevalence stabilizes
  • Complications can be serious due to comorbidities

Precautions | Preventions

  • Drink plenty of fluids, especially water, to dilute urine
  • Limit salt, animal protein, and oxalate-rich foods (spinach, nuts)
  • Maintain healthy body weight and exercise regularly
  • Avoid excessive vitamin D or calcium supplements unless prescribed
  • Monitor and manage medical conditions affecting metabolism
  • Follow your doctor’s advice on diet and medications

Treatment Options

Non-Surgical Treatment

  • Pain management with NSAIDs or opioids
  • Hydration to facilitate stone passage
  • Medications to relax ureter muscles (alpha-blockers)
  • Monitoring for stones likely to pass spontaneously

Surgical Treatment

  • Extracorporeal shock wave lithotripsy (ESWL) to break stones
  • Ureteroscopy with laser lithotripsy for stone removal
  • Percutaneous nephrolithotomy for large stones
  • Surgical removal in rare complicated cases

Comparison: Non-Surgical vs Surgical

Approach Benefits Limitations
Non-Surgical Pain relief, non-invasive, allows spontaneous stone passage Only suitable for small stones, may take days to weeks
Surgical Effective for larger or obstructive stones, quick resolution Invasive, risk of complications, requires recovery
⚠️ Immediate medical care is required for severe pain, infection, or urinary obstruction.

Ancient Treatments

Historically, kidney stones were treated with herbal medicines and dietary recommendations to reduce stone formation. Herbs such as jasminum, coriander, and celery seeds were used to aid kidney function and relieve pain.

Natural Remedies for Kidney Stones

Complementary natural approaches can support kidney health and reduce stone risk:

Dietary and Lifestyle Measures

  • Increase water intake to maintain dilute urine
  • Limit intake of oxalate-rich foods (nuts, spinach) and excess salt
  • Consume citrus fruits which may help prevent stones
  • Maintain healthy body weight and avoid dehydration

Herbal Remedies

  • Chanca piedra (stone breaker), horsetail, and dandelion root
  • Consult doctor before use, especially if on medications

Natural remedies support prevention and symptom relief but do not replace medical treatment in acute cases.
