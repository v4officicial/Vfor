Keratitis | Corneal Inflammation & Infections

Keratitis is the inflammation of the cornea, the clear outer layer of the eye that protects and helps focus vision. It can result from infections (bacteria, viruses, fungi, or parasites), injury, or misuse of contact lenses. If untreated, keratitis can lead to corneal ulcers, scarring, or even vision loss.

Symptoms

Keratitis symptoms vary depending on the cause but commonly include:

  • Eye redness and irritation
  • Pain or discomfort in the eye
  • Blurred or decreased vision
  • Sensitivity to light (photophobia)
  • Excessive tearing or discharge
  • Sensation of something stuck in the eye

Causes

Keratitis can occur due to infectious or non-infectious factors. Major causes include:

  • Bacterial infection (common in contact lens users)
  • Viral infection (Herpes simplex or Herpes zoster)
  • Fungal infection (from plant or soil injury)
  • Acanthamoeba parasites (linked with contaminated water & contact lenses)
  • Eye injury or foreign objects
  • Prolonged or improper use of contact lenses
  • Vitamin A deficiency

Children

  • Primarily linked to viral eye infections or injury
  • Less frequent compared to adults

Adults

  • Contact lens misuse is the major cause
  • Bacterial and viral keratitis are most prevalent

Elderly

  • Higher risk due to weakened immunity, dry eye syndrome, or eye surgeries
  • Reactivation of viral infections (Herpes zoster ophthalmicus)

Precautions | Prevention

  • Practice good contact lens hygiene
  • Avoid swimming with contact lenses in
  • Use protective eyewear during outdoor or manual work
  • Wash hands before touching your eyes
  • Avoid sharing towels, cosmetics, or eyedrops
  • Seek prompt treatment for any eye injury or infection

Treatment Options

Bacterial Keratitis

  • Antibiotic eye drops/ointment
  • Severe cases may require oral antibiotics

Viral Keratitis

  • Antiviral eye drops (e.g., for herpes keratitis)
  • Oral antiviral medication for recurrent infections

Fungal Keratitis

  • Antifungal eye drops (natamycin or voriconazole)
  • Severe cases require systemic antifungals

Parasitic (Acanthamoeba) Keratitis

  • Specialized anti-parasitic eye drops
  • May require long-term treatment due to resistance
⚠️ Early diagnosis is critical. Untreated keratitis can cause corneal scarring or permanent vision loss.

Types & Stages of Keratitis

Keratitis is classified based on its cause. The most common types include bacterial, viral, fungal, and parasitic keratitis. Each type of keratitis varies in severity, treatment options, and risk of complications. Prompt diagnosis is essential to protect corneal health and prevent vision loss.

Main Types of Keratitis

  • Bacterial Keratitis: Caused by bacteria such as Pseudomonas or Staphylococcus, mostly in contact lens wearers.
  • Viral Keratitis: Often caused by Herpes simplex virus or Herpes zoster virus. Can recur and damage the cornea over time.
  • Fungal Keratitis: Results from injury by soil, plants, or trauma. Fungi like Fusarium and Aspergillus are common culprits.
  • Acanthamoeba Keratitis: A rare but severe parasitic corneal infection, strongly linked to contact lens misuse or exposure to dirty water.

Stages of Keratitis

Stage Key Features Symptoms
Early Stage Mild corneal irritation, redness, tearing, light sensitivity Sensation of foreign body, mild blurred vision
Intermediate Stage Corneal infiltration with white/gray spots (ulcer formation) Pain, worsening blurred vision, discharge
Advanced Stage Deep corneal ulcer, thinning cornea, risk of perforation Severe vision loss, scarring, possible blindness if untreated

Natural Remedies for Keratitis

While natural methods cannot cure keratitis, they may support healing alongside medical care:

Dietary Choices

  • Vitamin A-rich foods (carrots, spinach, sweet potatoes)
  • Immune-boosting fruits (citrus, berries)
  • Hydrating foods (cucumber, watermelon)

Lifestyle Support

  • Rest the eyes when inflamed
  • Use lubricating artificial tears
  • Avoid excessive screen time during infection

⚠️ Natural remedies only provide supportive care and cannot replace prescribed medical treatments.
