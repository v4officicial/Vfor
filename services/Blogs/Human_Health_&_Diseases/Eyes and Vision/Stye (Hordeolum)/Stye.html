Stye (Hordeolum) | Painful Eyelid Infection

A stye, medically called a hordeolum, is a bacterial infection causing a painful, red, swollen lump on the eyelid margin. It results from blockage and infection of the sebaceous or sweat glands of the eyelid. Most styes heal on their own with basic care, but some may require medical treatment to prevent complications.

Symptoms

Common symptoms of a stye include:

  • Painful red bump on the eyelid margin
  • Swelling and tenderness of the eyelid
  • Itching or irritation around the eye
  • Increased tearing or watery eyes
  • Pus or a yellowish spot on the bump
  • Sensitivity to light and discomfort blinking

Causes

A stye is primarily caused by bacterial infection, most commonly by Staphylococcus aureus. Factors that increase the risk include:

  • Poor eyelid hygiene or eye rubbing with dirty hands
  • Chronic blepharitis or inflammation of eyelid margin
  • Use of expired or contaminated eye makeup
  • Improper contact lens hygiene
  • Blocked or malfunctioning eyelid glands
  • Weakened immune system or diabetes

Precautions | Prevention

  • Maintain regular eyelid hygiene by cleaning lid margins
  • Avoid touching or rubbing eyes with unwashed hands
  • Remove eye makeup thoroughly before sleeping
  • Replace eye cosmetics every 3 months
  • Properly clean and disinfect contact lenses
  • Avoid sharing eye makeup or personal items

Treatment Options

Most styes resolve with conservative care, but some need medical intervention:

Home Care

  • Warm compresses applied to the eyelid 4 times daily for 10-15 minutes
  • Gentle eyelid massage after warm compress to promote drainage
  • Avoid squeezing or popping the stye
  • Maintain eyelid cleanliness during healing

Medical Treatment

  • Topical antibiotic ointments (erythromycin or bacitracin)
  • Oral antibiotics for spreading or recurrent infections
  • Incision and drainage for large, persistent, or rapidly worsening styes
  • Pain relief with over-the-counter analgesics
⚠️ Consult your eye doctor if redness or swelling spreads beyond the eyelid, or if the stye fails to improve within 7-10 days.

Types & Stages of Stye (Hordeolum)

Styes can be classified as external or internal based on which eyelid glands are infected. Understanding the type helps determine treatment and prognosis.

Types of Stye

  • External Stye: Infection of the sebaceous (Zeis) or sweat (Moll) glands at the eyelid margin, appearing as a small red bump near the eyelashes.
  • Internal Stye: Infection of the meibomian glands located inside the eyelid, causing a deeper, tender swelling on the inner eyelid surface.

Stages of Stye Development

Stage Key Features Symptoms
Early Stage Redness, tenderness, and swelling begins Pain, itching, mild eyelid discomfort
Progression Pustule formation with possible yellow pus point Increased pain, swelling, and visible lump
Resolution Bump bursts or resolves with discharge; healing starts Reduced pain and swelling, healing of infected area

Comparison of External vs Internal Stye

Feature External Stye Internal Stye
Location Eyelid margin near eyelashes Inside eyelid, meibomian gland
Appearance Small red bump with visible pus head Larger lump, deeper swelling
Pain Usually less intense Tends to be more painful and tender
Treatment Warm compresses & topical antibiotics May require oral antibiotics and possible drainage
Complications Rare, usually self-limiting More risk of chalazion formation or spreading infection

Natural Remedies for Stye

Natural remedies may help relieve symptoms and support healing but should complement medical care:

At-Home Care

  • Warm compresses to improve blood flow and promote drainage
  • Gentle eyelid washing with mild baby shampoo or saline solution
  • Avoiding eye makeup and contact lens use during healing
  • Consuming a balanced diet rich in vitamins A, C, and zinc for eye health

⚠️ Natural remedies aid symptom relief and healing but do not replace antibiotics or professional care for infected styes.
