Conjunctivitis | Pink Eye Infection

Conjunctivitis, commonly known as pink eye, is an inflammation of the conjunctiva — the thin, transparent tissue covering the white part of the eye and the inner surface of eyelids. It leads to redness, irritation, watering, and discharge in one or both eyes. It can be caused by viruses, bacteria, allergies, or irritants, and is highly contagious in many cases.

Symptoms

Common symptoms of conjunctivitis include:

  • Redness in the white part of the eye
  • Itching, burning, or gritty feeling in the eye
  • Watery or thick, sticky discharge
  • Crusting of eyelids, especially in the morning
  • Tearing and light sensitivity
  • Swollen eyelids

Causes

Conjunctivitis can develop due to infections, allergies, or irritants. Main causes are:

  • Viral infections like adenoviruses (most common and highly contagious)
  • Bacterial infections (e.g., Staphylococcus, Streptococcus)
  • Allergic reactions to pollen, dust, or pet dander
  • Exposure to smoke, chlorine, or chemical irritants
  • Contact lens overuse or improper hygiene

Children

  • Viral conjunctivitis spreads quickly in schools and daycare
  • Bacterial infections are common due to poor hygiene
  • Allergic conjunctivitis triggered by seasonal allergies

Adults

  • Workplace exposure to irritants
  • Allergy-related conjunctivitis
  • Vision correction device misuse (contact lenses)

Aged People

  • Compromised immunity increases infection risk
  • Chronic allergic or dry eye may lead to repeated infections
  • Medication-induced irritation of the eyes

Precautions | Prevention

  • Wash hands frequently and avoid touching the eyes
  • Do not share towels, pillows, or cosmetics
  • Clean contact lenses properly and avoid wearing them during infection
  • Use protective glasses in dusty or chemical-exposed environments
  • Maintain hygiene in schools and crowded places

Treatment Options

Medical Treatment

  • Viral conjunctivitis: Usually self-limiting, managed with cold compresses and lubricating drops.
  • Bacterial conjunctivitis: Treated with antibiotic eye drops or ointments prescribed by an eye doctor.
  • Allergic conjunctivitis: Relieved with antihistamine or anti-allergy eye drops.

Home Remedies

  • Apply cold or warm compresses to reduce swelling and irritation
  • Clean discharge with a clean, damp cloth
  • Avoid contact lenses until infection clears
  • Stay in a clean, dust-free environment

⚠️ If conjunctivitis symptoms persist beyond a week or worsen, medical attention is necessary. Self-medication without a diagnosis can worsen eye health.

Ancient Treatments

In traditional practices, conjunctivitis was managed with soothing herbal washes like rosewater or turmeric-infused solutions. Ayurvedic and Unani medicine often used cooling and anti-inflammatory herbs. While they may provide comfort, these remedies should not replace sterile, medically-approved treatments.

Natural Remedies for Conjunctivitis

Natural remedies do not cure conjunctivitis but can support healing and reduce discomfort.

Common Natural Approaches

  • Cold compress with clean water
  • Cucumber slices placed on closed eyelids
  • Chamomile tea bag compresses
  • Aloe vera gel around, not inside, the eye

Dietary Support

  • Increase Vitamin C-rich foods to strengthen immunity
  • Consume omega-3 fatty acids for eye health
  • Stay hydrated to reduce dryness and irritation

⚠️ Natural remedies may help ease symptoms, but bacterial and complicated cases require medical treatment. Avoid applying unsterile substances directly inside the eye.
