Dry Eyes | Eye Moisture Imbalance

Dry eye syndrome, also known as keratoconjunctivitis sicca, occurs when your eyes do not produce enough quality tears to stay lubricated. It leads to discomfort, burning sensations, eye fatigue, and blurry vision. It has become increasingly common due to screen exposure, aging, and environmental factors.

Symptoms

Most common symptoms of dry eyes include:

  • Burning, stinging, or scratchy sensation in the eyes
  • Red eyes with occasional tearing
  • Feeling of “something in the eye”
  • Difficulty wearing contact lenses
  • Blurry vision, especially after screen use
  • Eye fatigue or discomfort in dry environments

Causes

Dry eyes occur when there is inadequate tear production or poor tear quality. Causes include:

  • Aging (tear production declines with age)
  • Prolonged screen time reducing blink rate
  • Exposure to dry air, wind, or smoke
  • Long-term use of contact lenses
  • Autoimmune disorders (like Sjögren’s syndrome)
  • Side effects of medications such as antihistamines, blood pressure drugs
  • Eye surgeries such as LASIK

Children

  • Prolonged digital device usage
  • Vitamin A deficiency
  • Environmental dryness (air conditioning, pollution)

Adults

  • Work-related screen exposure
  • Contact lens wearers at higher risk
  • Dry climates and occupational hazards

Elderly

  • Age-related decline in tear gland function
  • Higher risk with chronic diseases (diabetes, arthritis)
  • Medication-induced dryness more common

Precautions | Prevention

  • Limit screen time and take regular blinking breaks
  • Use a humidifier in dry environments
  • Wear wraparound sunglasses outdoors
  • Stay hydrated with plenty of fluids
  • Avoid smoke and direct air from fans or AC

Treatment Options

Medical Treatment

  • Artificial tears: Over-the-counter lubricating eye drops
  • Prescription drops: Cyclosporine (Restasis), lifitegrast (Xiidra)
  • Punctal plugs: Minimally invasive procedure to block tear drainage
  • Thermal pulsation therapy: Clears blocked glands for oil tear layer

Home Remedies

  • Warm compress to improve oil gland function
  • Omega-3 supplements for healthy tear production
  • Blinking exercises during long screen sessions
  • Balanced diet rich in vitamins A, C, E

Ancient Treatments

In Ayurveda and Siddha medicine, dry eyes were treated with ghee-based eye applications, Triphala washes, and cooling herbs. Traditional Chinese medicine used acupuncture and herbal teas to balance moisture. These methods provided relief but modern medicine offers more effective solutions.

Natural Remedies for Dry Eyes

Natural remedies can improve tear quality and reduce symptoms of mild dry eyes.

Diet & Nutrition

  • Include omega-3 fatty acids from flaxseed, chia seeds, fish oil
  • Eat leafy greens, carrots, and citrus fruits for eye vitamins
  • Stay hydrated with 8–10 glasses of water daily

Natural Soothing Remedies

  • Cold compress or rose water cotton pads
  • Aloe vera gel applied around eyelids (not inside)
  • Warm compress to unclog oil glands

⚠️ Avoid self-medicating with non-sterile products in the eye. Consult an ophthalmologist if symptoms persist or worsen.
