Fungal Nail Infections (Onychomycosis)

Fungal nail infections, medically called Onychomycosis, are chronic fungal infections affecting fingernails or toenails. They often cause discoloration, thickening, and brittleness of nails, leading to discomfort or cosmetic concerns. This infection is usually caused by dermatophytes, yeasts, or non-dermatophyte molds.

Symptoms

Symptoms of fungal nail infections may develop slowly and include:

  • White, yellow, or brown discoloration of the nail
  • Thickened or distorted nail shape
  • Brittle, crumbly, or ragged nails
  • Separation of the nail from the nail bed (onycholysis)
  • Foul odor from the infected nail
  • Pain or discomfort, especially when pressure is applied

Causes

Fungal nail infections occur when fungi invade the nail through tiny cracks or cuts. Common causative fungi and risk factors include:

  • Dermatophyte fungi such as Trichophyton rubrum
  • Yeasts like Candida species (mainly in fingernails)
  • Non-dermatophyte molds
  • Warm, moist environments such as sweaty shoes or communal showers
  • Injuries to the nail or nail diseases
  • Diabetes, poor circulation, or weakened immune system
  • Age over 60 years
  • Walking barefoot in damp public places

Children

  • Less common than in adults but possible due to exposure and nail trauma
  • More often caused by yeasts rather than dermatophytes

Adults

  • More common in toenails due to shoe use and trauma
  • Risk factors include athlete's foot, diabetes, and immunosuppression

Elderly

  • Higher prevalence due to slower nail growth and circulation problems
  • Often chronic and resistant to treatment

Precautions | Prevention

  • Keep feet clean and dry, changing socks regularly.
  • Wear breathable footwear and avoid tight shoes.
  • Avoid walking barefoot in communal showers or pools.
  • Trim nails regularly and avoid injury.
  • Disinfect nail grooming tools.
  • Treat athlete's foot promptly to avoid spread to nails.
  • Maintain good hygiene and manage chronic health issues.

Treatment Options

Topical Treatments

  • Ciclopirox nail lacquer applied daily for several months.
  • Efinaconazole and tavaborole topical solutions for mild to moderate cases.

Oral Treatments

  • Terbinafine oral tablets for 6-12 weeks (commonly used).
  • Itraconazole as pulse therapy for certain patients.
  • Monitoring liver function during oral antifungal therapy.

Other Treatments

  • Laser therapy for resistant infections (not universally available).
  • Removal of severely damaged nails in some cases.

Ancient Treatments

Traditional remedies for nail fungus included herbal pastes made from neem, garlic, or turmeric applied to nails. Sunlight exposure and soaking nails in vinegar or saltwater were also used to reduce fungal growth, though modern antifungal medications provide more effective and faster cures.

Natural Remedies for Fungal Nail Infections

Natural options can complement antifungal treatment by reducing fungal load and promoting nail health.

Supportive Natural Treatments

  • Tea tree oil applied carefully for antifungal properties.
  • Vinegar foot soaks to alter nail pH and inhibit fungi.
  • Coconut oil with antifungal effects applied to affected nails.
  • Lemon juice for antimicrobial and nail brightening benefits.

Natural remedies aid symptom control but are not substitutes for medical antifungal therapy.
