Skin Cancer | Melanoma & Non-Melanoma Types

Skin cancer is the abnormal growth of skin cells, primarily caused by ultraviolet (UV) radiation exposure from the sun or tanning beds. It is grouped mainly into melanoma and non-melanoma types. Melanoma is the most dangerous form, originating in pigment-producing melanocytes, while non-melanoma includes basal cell carcinoma and squamous cell carcinoma, usually less aggressive but still harmful if untreated.

Symptoms

Skin cancer symptoms vary by type but may include early warning signs such as changes in skin appearance and texture:

  • New, unusual growths or moles that change in size, shape, or color
  • Asymmetrical moles with irregular borders (common in melanoma)
  • Scaly, red patches or open sores that do not heal
  • Raised, pearly or waxy bumps (basal cell carcinoma)
  • Rough, thickened patches that may crust or bleed (squamous cell carcinoma)
  • Itchiness, tenderness, or pain at affected skin sites
  • Darkening or spreading of pigment around a mole

Causes

The primary cause of skin cancer is DNA damage to skin cells caused by UV radiation exposure. Risk factors include:

  • Excessive sun exposure and history of sunburns
  • Use of tanning beds and lamps
  • Fair skin with less melanin protection
  • Family history of skin cancer
  • Having many or unusual moles
  • Weakened immune system
  • Exposure to certain chemicals like arsenic
  • Age, as risk increases with time accumulated UV damage

Precautions | Preventions

  • Limit direct sun exposure especially between 10 AM and 4 PM.
  • Apply broad-spectrum sunscreen with SPF 30 or higher regularly.
  • Wear protective clothing, sunglasses, and wide-brimmed hats.
  • Avoid tanning beds and artificial UV sources.
  • Perform regular skin self-examinations to catch early signs.
  • Seek dermatologist evaluation for suspicious moles or spots.
  • Keep infants and children especially protected from direct sunlight.
  • Use shade whenever possible during outdoor activities.
  • Maintain a healthy immune system through balanced diet and lifestyle.
  • Be vigilant about medication side effects that increase sun sensitivity.

Treatment Options

Surgical Treatments

  • Excisional surgery: Complete removal of tumor with margin of healthy tissue.
  • Mohs micrographic surgery: Layer-by-layer removal ensuring cancer-free margins, especially in facial areas.
  • Curettage and electrodessication: Scraping and burning cancerous tissue for superficial lesions.

Non-Surgical Treatments

  • Topical medications: Chemotherapy creams like 5-fluorouracil, immune response modifiers like imiquimod.
  • Radiation therapy: Targeted radiation for inoperable tumors or patients unfit for surgery.
  • Systemic therapies: Immunotherapy, targeted therapy, and chemotherapy for advanced melanoma.

Emerging Treatments

  • Checkpoint inhibitors: Drugs that help immune system recognize and attack melanoma cells.
  • Targeted therapies: Medications directed at specific genetic mutations in melanoma cells.

Comparison: Melanoma vs. Non-Melanoma Treatment

Type Treatment Approach Prognosis
Melanoma Surgery, immunotherapy, targeted therapy for advanced stages. Can be life-threatening if not detected early; prognosis improves with early treatment.
Non-Melanoma Surgical removal, topical treatments, radiation for certain cases. Usually good prognosis; slower growing but requires treatment to avoid disfigurement.
⚠️ Early detection and treatment are critical to prevent spread and complications of skin cancer.

Ancient Treatments

Historically, skin cancers were treated with rudimentary methods including herbal poultices, cauterization, and plant-based applications. While these lacked scientific efficacy, some traditional herbs were noted for anti-inflammatory effects. Ancient medical practitioners often used natural resins and oils, but modern surgical and medical advances now provide effective cures.

Natural Remedies for Skin Cancer Support

Natural remedies can support overall skin health and may complement conventional treatments but are not substitutes for medical care in skin cancer.

Supportive Home Care

  • Consume antioxidant-rich foods such as berries, green tea, and leafy greens.
  • Use topical applications like aloe vera, green tea extracts, and turmeric for skin support.
  • Apply natural oils (e.g., coconut oil) to keep skin moisturized post-treatment.
  • Avoid irritants and allergens that can worsen skin condition.
  • Maintain good hydration and a balanced diet to support immune function.

Sun Protection

  • Use natural mineral-based sunscreens containing zinc oxide or titanium dioxide.
  • Wear UV-protective clothing and hats during outdoor exposure.

Consult healthcare providers before using natural remedies in conjunction with cancer treatments to avoid interactions or side effects.
