Non-Melanoma Skin Cancer | Basal Cell & Squamous Cell Carcinoma

Non-melanoma skin cancers (NMSC) primarily include basal cell carcinoma (BCC) and squamous cell carcinoma (SCC). These cancers develop in the skin's outer layers and are generally less aggressive than melanoma, but still require prompt treatment to prevent local tissue damage and rare metastasis.

Symptoms

Common symptoms vary by type but often involve changes in skin texture and appearance:

  • Basal cell carcinoma: Pearly, waxy bumps, often with visible blood vessels; may crust or bleed.
  • Squamous cell carcinoma: Scaly, red patches or thickened areas; open sores or warts that do not heal.
  • Persistent non-healing ulcer or sore on sun-exposed areas.
  • Raised growths or new skin lumps that slowly enlarge.
  • Itching, tenderness, or pain around lesions.

Causes

Non-melanoma skin cancers are primarily caused by DNA damage from UV radiation. Other risk factors include:

  • Long-term sun exposure and history of sunburns.
  • Age, with higher risk in older adults due to accumulated UV damage.
  • Fair skin, light hair, and eye color.
  • Immunosuppression due to medications or conditions.
  • Exposure to carcinogenic chemicals like arsenic or coal tar.
  • Chronic skin inflammation or scars.
  • Human papillomavirus (HPV) infection linked to some SCC cases.

Precautions | Prevention

  • Avoid excessive sun exposure especially peak UV hours.
  • Use high-SPF broad-spectrum sunscreen regularly.
  • Wear UV-protective hats, clothing, and sunglasses.
  • Avoid tanning beds and artificial UV sources.
  • Perform regular skin checks and report unusual lesions to dermatologists.
  • Protect scars or chronic wound areas from sun exposure.
  • Maintain healthy immune system with proper nutrition and lifestyle.
  • Stay informed about medications increasing sun sensitivity.

Treatment Options

Surgical Treatments

  • Excision: Surgical removal with clear margins.
  • Mohs micrographic surgery: Precise tumor removal preserving surrounding tissue.
  • Curettage and electrodessication: Scraping and cauterization for superficial lesions.

Non-Surgical Treatments

  • Topical therapies: Chemotherapy creams like 5-fluorouracil or immune response modifiers.
  • Radiation therapy: For patients not suitable for surgery or recurrent tumors.

Comparison: Basal vs. Squamous Cell Treatments

Type Treatment Approach Prognosis
Basal Cell Carcinoma (BCC) Usually surgery; topical or radiation for selected cases. High cure rates; rare metastasis; local tissue destruction possible.
Squamous Cell Carcinoma (SCC) Surgery mainstay; radiation as adjunct; aggressive cases need systemic therapy. Good prognosis if early; risk of metastasis higher than BCC.
⚠️ Early diagnosis and treatment prevent complications and possible spread.

Ancient Treatments

Ancient treatments for skin lesions included herbal ointments, cautery, and topical applications from plants. Remedies such as turmeric, aloe vera, and honey were used to soothe skin and reduce inflammation, though these were largely symptomatic relief without curing cancer.

Natural Remedies for Skin Health

While natural remedies do not cure non-melanoma skin cancer, they support skin healing and reduce irritation.

Supportive Natural Care

  • Aloe vera gel for soothing inflamed skin.
  • Turmeric paste for its anti-inflammatory and antioxidant properties.
  • Green tea extracts applied topically or consumed for antioxidant benefits.
  • Coconut oil or olive oil to moisturize and support skin barrier.
  • Vitamin-rich diet including vitamins A, C, and E to promote skin repair.

Sun Protection Strategies

  • Use mineral sunscreens with zinc oxide or titanium dioxide.
  • Wear protective clothing and hats to shield skin from UV rays.

Always coordinate natural remedies with healthcare providers to avoid interference with medical treatments.
