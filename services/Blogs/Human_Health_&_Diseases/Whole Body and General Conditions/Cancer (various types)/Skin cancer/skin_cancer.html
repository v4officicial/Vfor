Skin Cancer | Melanoma & Non-melanoma

Skin cancer is the abnormal and uncontrolled growth of skin cells, most often caused by UV exposure from sunlight or tanning beds. The most common types are basal cell carcinoma, squamous cell carcinoma, and melanoma. Early detection allows for simple, effective treatment and cure in most cases[web:74][web:76][web:77][web:79][web:81].

Symptoms

Warning signs and symptoms of skin cancer may include[web:75][web:76][web:77][web:79][web:82]:

  • New growth, mole, or spot on the skin
  • Changes in size, color, or shape of an existing mole
  • Painful, itchy, or bleeding spot or sore that does not heal
  • Red, scaly patches or bumps
  • Waxy, translucent, shiny, or pearly bump
  • Flat, firm, or pale area that looks like a scar
  • Dark streaks under fingernails or toenails (possible melanoma)
  • Rough surface or crusting, especially on sun-exposed areas
  • Persistent lesion with irregular border or color

Causes

Skin cancer usually starts in the epidermis (outer layer) due to DNA damage in skin cells caused by[web:79][web:77][web:83]:

  • Chronic exposure to sunlight (UV radiation)
  • Frequent sunburns during childhood/adulthood
  • Tanning bed use
  • Fair skin, light hair or eye color (less melanin)
  • Family history of skin cancer
  • Weakened immune system
  • Previous skin cancer or precancerous lesions
  • Chronic wounds or scars
  • Exposure to toxic chemicals (arsenic, coal tar)
  • Older age (risk increases after 50)

Children

  • Very rare in children
  • Risk factors include genetic conditions and strong family history
  • Childhood sunburn may increase melanoma risk later

Adults

  • Most common in adults over 30
  • Sun exposure at work/leisure is a major risk factor
  • Men are more likely to develop skin cancer on body/trunk; women more often on legs

Elderly

  • Incidence rises with age due to cumulative UV exposure
  • Delayed identification may lead to more advanced disease

Prevention & Precautions

  • Limit and protect against sun exposure, especially midday UV
  • Use broad-spectrum sunscreen (SPF 30+), reapplying every 2 hours
  • Wear hats, sunglasses, and protective clothing outdoors
  • Avoid tanning beds altogether
  • Self-examine skin monthly for new growth or changing moles
  • See a dermatologist for regular skin checks if at risk
  • Treat precancerous lesions early

Treatment Options

Creams & Topical Therapy

  • Prescription creams (e.g., imiquimod, fluorouracil) for superficial basal or squamous cell carcinoma[web:74]
  • Photodynamic therapy uses special light and photosensitizer cream for select lesions

Surgery

  • Standard excision removes tumor and safety margin
  • Mohs micrographic surgery for facial or recurring cancers
  • Curettage and electrodesiccation for small, superficial cancers
  • Cryotherapy (freezing) for early or superficial lesions[web:81][web:80]

Other Treatments

  • Radiation therapy (when surgery is not possible)
  • Chemotherapy for advanced or metastatic skin cancer[web:74][web:80]
  • Immunotherapy (e.g., PD-1 inhibitors) and targeted therapies for melanoma or advanced disease[web:74][web:77]

⚠️ Early treatment prevents spread and scarring. Non-healing spots, color change, or quickly growing moles should be checked immediately[web:74][web:77][web:76].

Ancient Treatments

Ancient healers used herbal poultices and cauterization for persistent sores, often with disfiguring results. Understanding of skin cancer as a disease evolved with modern surgery and microscopy.

Natural & Lifestyle Support

Natural options support skin health and healing but cannot cure cancer:

Diet and Lifestyle

  • Eat antioxidant-rich foods, avoid excessive processed/fried foods
  • Protect skin from UV; use sunscreen and protective clothing
  • Avoid smoking and limit alcohol
  • Hydrate well and care for healing skin after treatments

Herbal/Home Supports

  • Aloe vera and turmeric for soothing irritation after medical therapy
  • Tea tree oil, only as directed, for supportive skin care
  • Do not use "black salve" or unregulated home remedies—these may be dangerous

Important Note

Lifestyle measures help recovery but medical diagnosis and treatment are essential for any suspicious skin lesion. Early dermatologist evaluation saves lives.
