Prostate Cancer | Prostate Gland Carcinoma

Prostate cancer develops in the prostate, a walnut-sized gland in the male reproductive system. Most cases occur in men over 50 and are slow-growing, but early detection is key for best outcomes. Screening with PSA blood tests and digital rectal exam can help find cancer early[web:66][web:69][web:70][web:72].

Symptoms

Prostate cancer may develop with no early symptoms. More advanced cases can present[web:66][web:68][web:69][web:70][web:72]:

  • Difficulty starting or maintaining urination
  • Increased urge to urinate, especially at night (nocturia)
  • Weak or interrupted urine stream
  • Painful urination or ejaculation
  • Blood in urine (hematuria) or semen
  • Losing weight without trying
  • Fatigue and tiredness
  • Pain in the back, hips, or pelvic area
  • Reduced sexual function and erectile dysfunction
  • Accidental urine leaking or bone pain in advanced cancer

Causes

Specific causes are unknown, but major risk factors for prostate cancer include[web:66][web:68][web:69][web:70]:

  • Advanced age (most cases over 50-60 years)
  • Family history and genetic mutations (BRCA1/2, Lynch syndrome)
  • Unhealthy diets high in animal fat
  • African or African-Caribbean ancestry
  • Obesity and sedentary lifestyle
  • Chronic prostatitis or urinary tract inflammation
  • Chemical/environmental exposures (e.g., pesticides)

Children

  • Prostate cancer is virtually unknown in children
  • Any case in youth indicates genetic abnormalities

Adults

  • Incidence increases sharply over age 50
  • Family history and genetic factors are major contributors
  • Healthy lifestyle may reduce risk and late detection risks

Older Adults

  • Commonest cancer in older men
  • Comorbidities can affect symptoms and treatment decisions

Prevention & Precautions

  • Eat a plant-based diet rich in fruits and vegetables
  • Exercise regularly, maintain a healthy weight
  • Avoid smoking and limit alcohol intake
  • Routine PSA testing and annual physical exams after age 50 (or earlier with risk factors)
  • Manage chronic urinary tract health
  • Know your family history and seek genetic counseling if needed

Treatment Options

Active Surveillance/Observation

  • Appropriate for early-stage or slow-growing tumors
  • Includes regular PSA tests, biopsies, and monitoring[web:66][web:69]

Surgery

  • Radical prostatectomy removes the prostate, sometimes with pelvic lymph nodes
  • Appropriate for localized disease[web:66][web:69][web:70]

Radiation Therapy

  • External beam or brachytherapy (internal), sometimes in combination
  • Curative or palliative for advanced cases

Hormone Therapy

  • Medications or surgery to reduce testosterone
  • Used in advanced/metastatic cancer, or with other treatments[web:67]

Chemotherapy & Targeted Therapies

  • Needed for advanced or hormone-resistant cancer[web:67][web:68]
  • Targeted therapies (e.g., PARP inhibitors) for patients with genetic mutations

Comparison: Surgery vs. Hormone Therapy

Treatment Advantages Limitations
Surgery - Curative for early disease
- Removes primary tumor
- Reduces risk of local progression 		- Major operation with possible urinary/sexual side effects
- Not suitable if cancer has spread widely
- Requires general health fitness
Hormone Therapy - Controls advanced/metastatic cancer
- Non-surgical option
- Can be combined with other treatments 		- Side effects: sexual dysfunction, hot flashes, bone thinning
- Does not cure cancer, just controls progression
⚠️ Early detection provides the best chance of cure. Regular screening is vital, especially after age 50 or with family history[web:66][web:69][web:72].

Ancient Treatments

Historically, prostate problems were managed with catheterization, bladder flushing, or herbal medicines for urinary complaints. True cancer treatment became possible only with modern surgical and radiation advances.

Natural & Lifestyle Support

Natural approaches relieve symptoms and support well-being—not a cancer cure:

Diet and Lifestyle

  • Tomatoes, green tea, cruciferous vegetables provide antioxidants and possible protection
  • Pelvic floor exercises enhance bladder control
  • Meditation/mindfulness and stress management
  • Avoid processed and high-fat meats

Herbal Support

  • Saw palmetto for mild urinary symptoms
  • Soy isoflavones may support prostate health
  • Always discuss supplements with a doctor to prevent interactions

Important Note

Lifestyle measures support treatment but never replace medical management of prostate cancer.
