Rabies | Deadly Viral Neurological Infection

Rabies is a fatal viral infection caused by the rabies virus, typically transmitted via bites or scratches from infected animals such as dogs, bats, and wild mammals. The virus affects the central nervous system, causing brain inflammation and, without urgent post-exposure prophylaxis, death. Rabies is preventable with timely wound care and vaccination.

Symptoms

After exposure, symptoms usually appear 1–3 months later but can occur after weeks or years. Key rabies symptoms include:

  • Fever and headache (early symptoms)
  • Fatigue, malaise, and anxiety
  • At the bite site: pain, pins and needles, itching (paresthesia)
  • Agitation, confusion, hallucinations
  • Excessive salivation and difficulty swallowing
  • Hydrophobia (fear of water)
  • Aerophobia (fear of air drafts)
  • Muscle spasms and paralysis
  • Insomnia or altered mental status
  • Eventually, respiratory failure and coma

Causes

Rabies is caused by the rabies virus, transmitted via:

  • Bite, scratch, or lick from rabid animals (dogs, bats, foxes, raccoons, etc.)
  • Exposure to saliva through mucous membranes or open wounds
  • Rare: organ transplants from undiagnosed rabid donors

Human-to-human transmission is extremely rare. Unvaccinated pets and stray animals are common sources.

Children

  • High risk due to close contact with animals
  • May not disclose bites or scratches promptly
  • Early symptoms: fever, restlessness, and bite site discomfort

Adults

  • Exposure during outdoor work or recreation
  • Unvaccinated adults, especially in rural areas, are at risk

Aged People

  • Reduced immunity increases fatality risk
  • Late diagnosis may worsen neurological outcomes

Precautions | Prevention

  • Vaccinate all pets per guidelines
  • Avoid contact with unknown, stray, or wild animals
  • Educate children on safety around animals
  • Cover garbage and secure home to prevent animal entry
  • Seek immediate wound cleaning and prompt medical care after bites
  • Start post-exposure rabies vaccination as soon as possible
  • Travellers to high-risk regions should consider pre-exposure rabies vaccination

Treatment Options

Urgent Post-exposure Prophylaxis (PEP)

  • Immediate and thorough washing of the wound with soap and water for 15 minutes
  • Administer rabies vaccine and rabies immune globulin (RIG) as soon as possible
  • Follow full vaccine schedule as per protocol
  • Delayed or absent PEP almost always results in death

Palliative Care (Once Symptoms Develop)

  • Supportive hospital care only; no cure after symptoms appear
  • Manage pain, agitation, and respiratory symptoms
  • Ventilator support as needed

Pre-exposure Prophylaxis

  • Rabies vaccination for high-risk groups (veterinarians, animal handlers, travellers to endemic areas)

Ancient Treatments

In the past, rabies was misunderstood and managed with herbal applications, cauterization, superstitions, or ritual healing — none effective in preventing death. The discovery of rabies vaccine in the late 19th century revolutionized care.

Natural Remedies for Rabies

No natural or home remedies prevent or cure rabies after exposure. Only timely wound cleaning and medical intervention (PEP) save lives. Herbal antiseptics or turmeric may be used with immediate wound washing before hospital visit, but must not delay medical treatment.

  • Cleanse bite area thoroughly with soap and water
  • Apply antiseptic if available
  • Seek emergency care without delay

Note: Rabies is always fatal without timely post-exposure vaccination — medical treatment is the only option.
