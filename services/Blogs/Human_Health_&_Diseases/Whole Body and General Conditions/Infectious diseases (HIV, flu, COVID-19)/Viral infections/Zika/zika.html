Zika Virus | Mosquito-borne Flavivirus Infection

Zika is a viral infection caused by the Zika virus, a mosquito-borne flavivirus mainly transmitted by Aedes mosquitoes. Zika infection is usually mild, but poses a special risk to pregnant women as it can cause severe birth defects like microcephaly in newborns. The virus can also be transmitted sexually or from mother to fetus.

Symptoms

Symptoms typically appear 3–14 days after exposure and are generally mild, lasting 2–7 days:

  • Fever (usually low-grade)
  • Rash (red spots, often starting on face and spreading)
  • Joint pain, especially in hands and feet
  • Muscle aches
  • Conjunctivitis (red eyes, sometimes itchy or painful)
  • Headache
  • Malaise or general weakness
  • Pain behind the eyes

Causes

Zika is mainly transmitted by Aedes mosquitoes, especially Aedes aegypti and Aedes albopictus. Transmission can also occur through:

  • Sexual contact with an infected person
  • Mother-to-child transmission during pregnancy or childbirth
  • Blood transfusion (rare)

Outbreaks are common in tropical and subtropical regions.

Children

  • Most children have mild or no symptoms
  • In congenital cases, risk of microcephaly and neurodevelopmental disorders exists if mother was infected during pregnancy
  • Monitor newborns for signs of neurological issues

Adults

  • Muscle, joint pain, eye pain, headache, and rash common
  • Rare complications: Guillain-Barré syndrome (neuropathy) and blood disorders

Aged People

  • Symptoms remain mild in most cases
  • Underlying chronic illness may increase fatigue or complications
  • Rare for severe neurological effects, but need close monitoring

Precautions | Prevention

  • Use mosquito repellents containing DEET, IR3535, or picaridin
  • Wear long-sleeved clothing and pants, especially during day time
  • Sleep under mosquito nets or in screened rooms
  • Remove stagnant water sources near home
  • Use condoms or avoid sex with partners who traveled to Zika-affected areas
  • Pregnant women should avoid travel to Zika-endemic regions
  • Practice strict mosquito control measures, especially during outbreaks

Treatment Options

Supportive Care

  • No specific antiviral treatment is available for Zika
  • Rest and plenty of fluids
  • Paracetamol (acetaminophen) to reduce fever and pain
  • Avoid aspirin/NSAIDs until dengue is ruled out to reduce bleeding risk

Pregnancy Monitoring

  • Prenatal screening for Zika exposure and fetal ultrasound monitoring
  • Consultation with an obstetrician for suspected or confirmed maternal infection

Severe Cases

  • Hospital care for rare neurological or immune complications (e.g., Guillain-Barré syndrome)

Ancient Treatments

Before modern medicine, mosquito fevers were managed with herbal decoctions, cool compresses, neem and tulsi (holy basil) infusions, and bitter gourd juice to lower fever. However, these did not target the actual virus or prevent complications.

Natural Remedies for Zika

Natural remedies can support recovery but do not cure Zika. Always consult a doctor for diagnosis and management, especially during pregnancy.

Home Care & Support

  • Stay hydrated: drink water, coconut water, and fruit juices
  • Rest and sleep well to help immune system
  • Use lukewarm sponges to lower fever
  • Apply cool cloth to joints for pain relief

Herbal Support (Traditional)

  • Neem, tulsi, and giloy teas believed to reduce fever (supportive only)
  • Ginger or turmeric warm drinks for mild aches
  • Probiotics like yogurt to support gut health

Note: Natural remedies provide comfort, but medical attention is required for pregnant women, abnormal symptoms, or suspected complications.
