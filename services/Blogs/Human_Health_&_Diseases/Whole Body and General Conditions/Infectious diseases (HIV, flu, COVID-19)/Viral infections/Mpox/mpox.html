Mpox (Monkeypox) | Viral Infection with Skin Lesions

Mpox, previously known as monkeypox, is a contagious viral disease caused by the monkeypox virus. It is characterized by fever, swollen lymph nodes, and a distinctive rash that progresses from macules to vesicles and pustules. Mpox spreads primarily through close contact, respiratory droplets, and contact with contaminated materials. Vaccination and isolation help control outbreaks.

Symptoms

Symptoms usually develop 5–21 days after exposure and include:

  • Fever, chills, and headache
  • Swollen lymph nodes (lymphadenopathy)
  • Muscle aches and fatigue
  • Back pain
  • Skin rash appearing 1-3 days after fever onset
  • Rash progression: macules → papules → vesicles → pustules → scabs
  • Lesions may be painful or itchy
  • Possible mouth sores, eye redness or discharge

Causes

Mpox is caused by the monkeypox virus, an orthopoxvirus related to smallpox virus. The virus is transmitted via:

  • Direct contact with skin lesions or body fluids of infected persons
  • Respiratory droplets during prolonged face-to-face contact
  • Contact with contaminated clothing, bedding, or objects
  • Sexual contact, especially among men who have sex with men (MSM) in recent outbreaks
  • Animal bites or scratches in endemic areas (central and west Africa)

Children

  • At risk from household or close contacts with infection
  • Symptoms similar to adults but may be milder
  • Heightened surveillance needed to prevent spread in schools

Adults

  • More common in sexually active adults in current outbreaks
  • Symptoms include fever, rash, lymphadenopathy, and fatigue
  • Complications can include secondary infections, pneumonia, or hospitalization

Aged People

  • Potentially more severe disease and complications
  • Vaccination status influences susceptibility and outcomes
  • Close monitoring advised

Precautions | Prevention

  • Avoid close contact with infected persons or animals
  • Practice good hand hygiene regularly
  • Use personal protective equipment (PPE) when caring for patients
  • Isolate infected individuals until lesions heal
  • Clean and disinfect commonly touched surfaces and objects
  • Use smallpox or mpox vaccine for contacts or high-risk groups
  • Practice safe sex and reduce number of sexual partners during outbreaks

Treatment Options

Symptomatic Care

  • Relief of fever and pain using paracetamol or ibuprofen
  • Maintain hydration and nutrition
  • Antihistamines for itch relief

Antiviral Therapy

  • Tecovirimat (TPOXX) approved for use in severe cases or immunocompromised patients

Isolation and Infection Control

  • Patients should be isolated until lesions have healed and scabs have fallen off
  • Contact tracing and monitoring of exposed persons

Ancient Treatments

Before modern medicine, skin rash illnesses were treated with herbal poultices, cooling baths, and wound care with natural substances like neem and turmeric. These might have provided symptomatic relief but not disease control.

Natural Remedies for Mpox

Natural remedies may help soothe skin irritation but do not treat or cure Mpox. These can be used alongside medical care for comfort.

Skin Care

  • Cool compresses to relieve itching and inflammation
  • Aloe vera gel applied gently on lesions
  • Keep skin clean and dry to prevent secondary bacterial infections
  • Oatmeal baths to soothe irritation

General Support

  • Stay hydrated and maintain balanced nutrition
  • Rest adequately to boost immune response

Note: Avoid scratching lesions to prevent scarring and secondary infections. Seek medical care if symptoms worsen or complications develop.
