Tuberculosis (TB) | Mycobacterium tuberculosis Infection

Tuberculosis (TB) is a contagious bacterial infection caused by Mycobacterium tuberculosis, primarily affecting the lungs but can infect other parts of the body. TB spreads through airborne droplets when a person with active TB coughs or sneezes. Early diagnosis and rigorous treatment are critical to managing TB and preventing spread.[web:59][web:62][web:64]

Symptoms

Symptoms differ between latent (inactive) and active TB. Active TB symptoms vary by site but commonly include:[web:59][web:62]

  • Persistent cough lasting more than 3 weeks, sometimes with blood-tinged sputum
  • Chest pain and difficulty breathing
  • Unexplained weight loss and fatigue
  • Fever, chills, and night sweats
  • Loss of appetite
  • Swollen lymph nodes, particularly in the neck
  • Symptoms vary with extrapulmonary TB (kidneys, spine, brain)

Causes

TB is caused by the inhalation of droplets containing Mycobacterium tuberculosis from an infected person with active pulmonary TB.[web:64][web:62]

  • Transmission via airborne droplets from coughing, sneezing, or speaking close to others
  • Close prolonged contact in crowded or poorly ventilated environments increases risk
  • Immunocompromised individuals (HIV, diabetes, malnutrition) are at higher risk of progressing to active TB
  • Latent TB infection remains asymptomatic but can reactivate when immunity wanes

Children

  • Often develop latent TB, with risk of rapid progression to active TB if untreated
  • May have non-specific symptoms such as failure to thrive, cough, or fever

Adults

  • Typical presentation includes chronic cough, weight loss, night sweats, and fatigue
  • Risk factors include HIV infection, smoking, diabetes, and alcohol use

Aged People

  • Higher risk of reactivation of latent TB due to immunosenescence
  • May present atypically with less fever or respiratory symptoms

Precautions | Prevention

  • Early identification and isolation of infectious cases to prevent transmission
  • BCG vaccination offers some protection in children
  • Improve ventilation and avoid close contact with active TB patients
  • Promote use of masks in healthcare or outbreak settings
  • Screen high-risk populations for latent TB and treat as indicated
  • Maintain good nutrition and manage conditions compromising immunity

Treatment

Standard Treatment

  • Antitubercular therapy (ATT) combines multiple antibiotics: isoniazid, rifampicin, pyrazinamide, and ethambutol typically for 6 months[web:62][web:64]
  • Directly observed therapy (DOTS) ensures adherence and prevents resistance development
  • Treatment for latent TB involves isoniazid or rifampin courses to prevent progression[web:64]
  • Adherence and monitoring of side effects are critical to treatment success

Multidrug-resistant TB (MDR-TB)

  • MDR-TB requires prolonged therapy with second-line agents, often less effective and more toxic[web:64]
  • Early detection and treatment are crucial to control outbreaks

Supportive Care

  • Nutrition, symptomatic relief (antipyretics, pain control), and monitoring of complications
  • Psychosocial support and education for patients and families

Comparison: Latent vs Active TB Treatment

Condition Treatment Focus Duration and Approach
Latent TB Prevent progression to active disease Short course of monotherapy or dual therapy (e.g., 3-9 months)
Active TB Eradicate bacteria and cure infection 6 months of multi-drug therapy under close supervision
⚠️ Treatment completion is essential to prevent resistance and relapse.[web:62][web:64]

Ancient Treatments

Before antibiotics, ancient remedies involved herbal concoctions, dietary modifications, and isolation. Ayurvedic texts described consumption of medicinal herbs, while respiratory therapies like steam inhalation eased breathing difficulty.[web:53]

Natural Remedies for Tuberculosis Support

Natural remedies alone cannot cure TB but may support general health and immune function when used alongside medical treatment.[web:54][web:62]

Diet and Supplements

  • Nutritious, high-protein diet to support immune defense and recovery
  • Herbs like turmeric, ginger, and garlic for anti-inflammatory and antimicrobial properties
  • Vitamin D and probiotics are being studied for adjunct benefits

Lifestyle Measures

  • Adequate rest and avoiding stress to aid healing
  • Good ventilation and hygiene to prevent spread
