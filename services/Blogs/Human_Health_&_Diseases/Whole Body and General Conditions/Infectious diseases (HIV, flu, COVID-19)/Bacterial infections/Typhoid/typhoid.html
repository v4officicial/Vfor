Typhoid Fever | Diagnosis & Treatment Overview

Diagnosis

The gold standard for diagnosing typhoid fever is blood culture, though it detects about 50% of cases and requires several days to produce results.[web:97][web:98][web:96]

  • Blood culture detects live Salmonella typhi bacteria but has limited sensitivity and takes 3-7 days.
  • Bone marrow culture is more sensitive (~80%) but invasive and less used.[web:97]
  • Stool and urine cultures have lower sensitivity early in illness but important for carrier detection.
  • Rapid antibody tests (Typhidot, Typhipoint EIA, TUBEX) detect IgM and IgG antibodies but vary in sensitivity and specificity.[web:96][web:98]
  • The Widal test measures agglutinating antibodies but is neither very specific nor sensitive and is often misleading in endemic areas.[web:96][web:98]
  • Clinical diagnosis remains important due to test limitations, supported by fever history, abdominal pain, and epidemiological context.[web:97]

Symptoms

Classic symptoms appear 6-30 days after infection and include:[web:97][web:101]

  • Prolonged high fever
  • Abdominal pain, cramps, and diarrhea or constipation
  • Headache, malaise, weakness
  • Enlarged liver and spleen in some patients
  • Rose-colored rash (rose spots) on chest or abdomen
  • Possible complications include intestinal perforation or hemorrhage if untreated

Causes

Typhoid fever is caused by ingestion of food or water contaminated with *Salmonella enterica* serotype Typhi bacteria.[web:97][web:96]

  • Fecal contamination of drinking water or food
  • Poor sanitation, inadequate sewage disposal, and food hygiene
  • Carrier state in some individuals who excrete bacteria without symptoms

Precautions | Prevention

  • Drink safe, boiled, or treated water; avoid unwashed raw foods.[web:65]
  • Maintain proper hand hygiene before eating or food preparation.
  • Avoid street food or unpasteurized dairy products.
  • Sanitation improvements and sewage control to prevent contamination.
  • Vaccination for travelers to endemic areas and high-risk populations.
  • Proper education on food safety and hygiene measures.[web:65]

Treatment

Typhoid fever is treated primarily with antibiotics to eliminate the bacteria and supportive care to manage symptoms and prevent complications.[web:97][web:100]

  • Antibiotics: ciprofloxacin, ceftriaxone, azithromycin depending on local resistance patterns.
  • Hydration and electrolyte replacement for diarrhea and fever.
  • Rest and symptom relief with antipyretics and pain medication.
  • Monitoring for complications like intestinal perforation or encephalopathy.
  • Completion of full antibiotic course essential to prevent relapse and resistance.[web:97][web:98]

Ancient Treatments

Traditional practices included herbal teas, fasting, poultice application, and dietary modifications to manage fever and gastrointestinal symptoms prior to modern antibiotics.[web:96][web:99]

Natural Remedies

Natural remedies may complement medical treatment by aiding hydration, immune support, and symptom relief but cannot replace antibiotics.[web:70][web:73]

  • Ginger and turmeric teas for anti-inflammatory and digestive relief.
  • Probiotic yogurt to support gut health after antibiotic use.
  • Oral rehydration solution made at home for electrolyte balance.
  • Honey for throat soothing and mild antimicrobial effect.
  • Maintain a nutritious, balanced diet to support recovery.
