Tuberculosis (TB) | Lung Infection

Tuberculosis (TB) is a potentially serious infectious disease caused by the bacterium Mycobacterium tuberculosis. It most commonly affects the lungs (pulmonary TB) but can also affect other parts of the body. Though treatable and preventable, TB remains a major global health issue, especially in developing countries.

Symptoms

Symptoms of active TB differ from latent TB (where the bacteria are present but inactive). Common signs of active pulmonary TB include:

  • Persistent cough lasting more than 3 weeks
  • Coughing up blood or sputum
  • Chest pain or discomfort
  • Unexplained weight loss
  • Loss of appetite
  • Night sweats and chills
  • Fever and fatigue
  • Swelling in the neck (due to TB in lymph nodes)

Causes

TB is caused by the airborne bacterium *Mycobacterium tuberculosis*. The bacteria spread through droplets released in the air when someone with active TB coughs, sneezes, or speaks. Factors increasing risk include:

  • Close contact with an active TB patient
  • Weakened immune system (HIV, diabetes, malnutrition)
  • Living in crowded or poorly ventilated conditions
  • Drug or alcohol abuse
  • Long-term medication such as corticosteroids lowering immunity
  • Lack of vaccination with BCG in early childhood

Children

  • Exposure to infected family members
  • Malnutrition lowering immunity
  • Lack of BCG vaccination

Adults

  • Working in healthcare or crowded environments
  • Chronic illnesses like diabetes or HIV
  • Alcohol abuse or smoking

Aged People

  • Declining immunity with aging
  • Reactivation of latent TB
  • Multiple health conditions weakening resistance

Precautions | Preventions

  • Get BCG vaccination for children in endemic regions.
  • Avoid close contact with patients having active TB.
  • Maintain well-ventilated living environments.
  • Use masks when visiting TB-prone areas or hospitals.
  • Complete the full TB treatment to prevent drug resistance.
  • Adopt a balanced diet to strengthen immunity.

Treatment Options

Medical Approaches

  • First-line anti-TB drugs: isoniazid, rifampicin, pyrazinamide, ethambutol
  • Treatment duration usually 6–9 months
  • Directly Observed Treatment, Short-course (DOTS) for adherence
  • Second-line drugs for drug-resistant TB

For Severe / Drug-Resistant Cases

  • Longer treatment with stronger drugs (fluoroquinolones, injectables)
  • Surgery in rare cases of lung damage
⚠️ Incomplete treatment can lead to Multi-Drug Resistant TB (MDR-TB), a major global health threat.

Ancient Treatments

In Ayurveda, tuberculosis was referred to as “Rajayakshma” and described as a wasting disease. Traditional remedies included herbs like Ashwagandha, tulsi, turmeric, and pippali, along with milk and ghee-based formulations. However, these remedies only provided supportive care and were not curative against the bacterial infection.

Natural Remedies for Tuberculosis

Natural remedies support treatment by improving nutrition and immunity but should never replace anti-TB medication.

Diet and Lifestyle Approaches

  • High-protein diet with eggs, milk, and legumes
  • Vitamin D and C rich foods for faster recovery
  • Rest and proper sleep to strengthen immunity

Herbal and Home Remedies

  • Garlic for natural antibacterial support
  • Turmeric as an anti-inflammatory agent
  • Ashwagandha for immune boosting
  • Tulsi decoction for respiratory health

Additional Measures

  • Sunlight exposure for Vitamin D production
  • Avoid alcohol and smoking
  • Isolation of contagious patients during early treatment

⚠️ Natural remedies are supportive; completing full TB medication under supervision is mandatory.
