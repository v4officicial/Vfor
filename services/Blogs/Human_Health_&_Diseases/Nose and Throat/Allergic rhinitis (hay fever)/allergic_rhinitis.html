Allergic Rhinitis (Hay Fever) | Nasal Allergy

Allergic Rhinitis, also known as hay fever, occurs when the immune system overreacts to airborne allergens such as pollen, dust, pet dander, and mold. It causes nasal congestion, sneezing, itchy eyes, runny nose, and fatigue. The condition can be seasonal (common in spring and fall due to pollen) or perennial (year-round due to dust or mites).

Symptoms

Common symptoms of allergic rhinitis include:

  • Frequent sneezing, especially in the morning
  • Runny or stuffy nose
  • Itchy, watery, or red eyes
  • Itchy throat, ears, or nose
  • Postnasal drip leading to cough
  • Headache and sinus pressure
  • Sleep disturbances due to congestion
  • Fatigue and difficulty concentrating

Causes

Allergic rhinitis is triggered when the immune system mistakenly identifies harmless airborne particles as threats, releasing histamine and other chemicals that cause inflammation and allergy symptoms.

  • Pollen (trees, grasses, weeds) – seasonal allergic rhinitis
  • House dust mites
  • Mold spores in damp areas
  • Animal dander (cats, dogs, birds)
  • Air pollution and cigarette smoke (aggravating factors)
  • Strong odors, perfumes, household cleaning products

Precautions | Prevention

  • Keep windows closed during high pollen seasons.
  • Use air purifiers and vacuum regularly to reduce dust mites.
  • Shower and change clothes after outdoor exposure to pollen.
  • Wear masks or sunglasses to minimize allergen exposure outdoors.
  • Wash bedding in hot water weekly to kill dust mites.
  • Avoid exposure to pets if allergic.
  • Limit outdoor activity in early morning and windy days (high pollen count).
  • Stay hydrated to thin mucus and reduce congestion.

Treatment Options

Lifestyle and Home Care

  • Avoid allergens whenever possible.
  • Perform saline nasal irrigation or rinses.
  • Use HEPA filters in living areas and bedrooms.
  • Practice yoga and breathing exercises to improve nasal flow.

Medication Support

  • Antihistamines (cetirizine, loratadine, fexofenadine)
  • Nasal corticosteroid sprays (fluticasone, mometasone)
  • Decongestants for short-term relief
  • Leukotriene receptor antagonists (montelukast in select cases)

Advanced Options

  • Allergen Immunotherapy (allergy shots or sublingual tablets)
  • Biologic therapies in cases of severe allergic asthma + rhinitis
⚠️ Seek medical evaluation if symptoms are severe, persistent, or interfere with sleep and daily activities.

Ancient Treatments

Ancient medicine for nasal allergies included herbal decoctions, steam inhalation with medicinal leaves, honey-based remedies, and Ayurvedic approaches like *Nasya* (medicated oil applied through the nostrils). Traditional Chinese medicine used acupuncture and herbal teas to improve immune balance. While not fully scientific, some still find them beneficial as complementary care.

Natural Remedies for Allergic Rhinitis

Natural remedies help in reducing the severity of allergic rhinitis and improving breathing comfort.

Diet and Lifestyle Approaches

  • Consume probiotics (yogurt, kefir) to strengthen immunity.
  • Include foods rich in vitamin C (lemon, oranges, bell peppers) to act as natural antihistamines.
  • Drink herbal teas such as ginger, green tea, or chamomile to reduce inflammation.

Herbal and Home Remedies

  • Steam inhalation with eucalyptus or peppermint oil.
  • Turmeric milk for anti-inflammatory benefits.
  • Honey (especially local raw honey) to reduce seasonal pollen sensitivity.
  • Quercetin-rich foods (apples, onions, leafy greens) to reduce histamine release.

Additional Measures

  • Regular nasal cleaning with saline spray.
  • Use essential oils (lavender, eucalyptus) as natural decongestants.
  • Maintain adequate sleep and stress management practices like meditation.

⚠️ Natural remedies work best for mild cases. Chronic or worsening symptoms should be managed with medical treatment.
